Historical Volatility

PART 1: MAIN GUIDE

1. Identity

Full Name: Historical Volatility

Abbreviation: HV

Category: Volatility / Risk Management

Creator: Black & Scholes (Concept popularizers in options pricing, 1973)

One-Sentence Definition: Historical Volatility measures the annualized standard deviation of logarithmic price returns, quantifying how wildly prices have fluctuated in the recent past.

2. Calculation Logic

Formula

StepFormulaPurpose
Log Returns$R_t = \ln\left(\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}\right)$Daily % change
Std Deviation$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(R_t - \bar{R})^2}{N-1}}$Dispersion measure
Annualize$HV = \sigma \times \sqrt{252} \times 100$Yearly scale

Variables:

- $P_t$ = Today's closing price

- $P_{t-1}$ = Yesterday's closing price

- $N$ = Lookback period (typically 20)

- $252$ = Trading days per year (for stocks)

Step-by-Step Logic:

1. Calculate log returns: $\ln(Close_t / Close_{t-1})$

2. Compute mean of log returns over $N$ periods

3. Calculate standard deviation using sample formula (N-1)

4. Annualize by multiplying by $\sqrt{252}$

5. Express as percentage (× 100)

Real-World Analogy

Think of Historical Volatility like a seismograph measuring market tremors. Low HV = the market is calm, like a sleeping volcano. High HV = the market is shaking violently, like an active eruption. Unlike price direction, HV tells you the intensity of movement, not which way it's going.

4. Market Context

Best: Pre-breakout squeeze detection, panic bottom identification

Good: Options pricing, risk management decisions

Worst: Steady grinding trends (HV stays moderate, no actionable signal)

Critical Insight: HV is NON-DIRECTIONAL. It tells you intensity, not direction. Always pair with price action or directional indicators.

7. Practical Cautions

No Direction: HV is non-directional. A rising HV could mean breakout UP or DOWN. Always pair with price breakout indicators.
Ghost Effect: A single massive gap creates an HV spike that lingers for exactly $N$ days, then drops instantly when the gap leaves the window. This "ghost" distorts percentile rankings.
Annualization Factor: Ensure correct constant: 252 for stocks, 365 for crypto, 250 for forex. Wrong factor = meaningless values.
Percentile > Absolute: Raw HV numbers vary wildly by asset class. Use HV Rank/Percentile for cross-asset comparison.

8. Pro Insights

The "Implied vs. Historical" Gap (Options Edge):

Options traders compare HV to IV (Implied Volatility from options prices):

- HV > IV: Market movement is GREATER than priced. Options are "cheap." → Buy straddles

- IV > HV: Market calm but fear is priced. Options are "expensive." → Sell premium

Volatility Cycles (Mandelbrot's Observation):

Volatility clusters. High volatility begets high volatility (crisis periods). Low volatility begets low volatility (complacency). But eventually, regimes change—the longer the calm, the bigger the eventual storm.

Percentile Ranking Solution:

Don't compare raw HV across assets. Use:

$$HV_{Rank} = \frac{Current\ HV - Min\ HV_{252}}{Max\ HV_{252} - Min\ HV_{252}}$$

This normalizes to 0-100% for any asset.

9. Summary

"Historical Volatility is your market's seismograph—it doesn't predict earthquakes, but it tells you whether the ground is stable or shaking, so you can prepare accordingly."

PART 2: SUPPLEMENTARY GUIDE

1. Core Concept

Historical Volatility answers: "How much has this asset been moving recently?"

HV measures the dispersion of returns—how far daily moves deviate from the average. High HV = wild swings. Low HV = quiet creeping. This has nothing to do with direction, only intensity.

2. Statistical Edge

The HV provides:

- Early warning of regime changes via volatility cycles

- Squeeze detection: 70%+ of major moves start from HV extremes

- Options pricing edge when compared to implied volatility

- Risk-adjusted position sizing (lower HV = larger positions)

3. Actionable Strategy: "Volatility Expansion Breakout"


STRATEGY: The Squeeze Breakout
SETUP: HV(20) drops to 6-month low (or below 10%)
TRIGGER: Price breaks 20-day Donchian Channel (new 20-day high or low)
DIRECTION: Direction of Donchian break
ENTRY: Close of breakout candle
STOP: Opposite Donchian band
TARGET: 2× ATR from entry (expansion target)

4. Risks & Limitations

- No direction: Must combine with breakout indicators

- Ghost spikes: Single gaps distort readings for N periods

- Asset-specific levels: 30% HV is panic for S&P, normal for Bitcoin

- Lagging in fast regimes: Takes time to reflect new volatility

5. Further Study

Creator: Used extensively in Black-Scholes options model (1973); John Bollinger popularized volatility cycles

Related: Implied Volatility (IV), Average True Range, VIX, Bollinger Band Width

Signal Patterns

HV Squeeze Buy Signal

This signal identifies the "volatility squeeze" setup where HV reaches near its 252-day minimum and price simultaneously breaks the Donchian channel. Low volatility environments store energy that often releases in explosive moves. 70%+ of major price moves start from extreme HV compression conditions.


def hv_squeeze_buy(hv, hv_min_252, price, donchian_high, prev_price):
    is_squeeze = hv <= hv_min_252 * 1.1  # Within 10% of 252-day low
    breakout = price > donchian_high and prev_price <= donchian_high
    return is_squeeze and breakout

HV Climax Exit Signal

This pattern detects when HV reaches extreme highs (90th percentile) and begins contracting—a sign of volatility exhaustion. Extreme volatility clusters tend to mean-revert, and the turning point often marks trend exhaustion. This signal warns traders to exit directional positions before the inevitable reversal or consolidation.


def hv_climax_exit(hv, hv_percentile):
    extreme_high = hv_percentile > 90
    turning_down = hv < hv[1]
    return extreme_high and turning_down

// Volatility Squeeze Scanner
