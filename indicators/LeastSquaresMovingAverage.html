Least Squares Moving Average (LSMA)

PART 1: MAIN GUIDE

1. Identity

Full Name: Least Squares Moving Average

Abbreviation: LSMA (also called Linear Regression Moving Average)

Category: Trend Following / Regression

Creator: Statistical derivation (applied to finance)

One-Sentence Definition: LSMA calculates the endpoint of the linear regression line over a lookback period, creating a moving average that reduces lag by "leaning into" the trend direction.

2. Calculation Logic

Formula

For each bar, LSMA fits a straight line $y = mx + b$ through the past $N$ closing prices using the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) method, then returns the endpoint of that line.

StepFormulaPurpose
1Sum $x$ (time indices: $1, 2, ..., N$)Time dimension
2Sum $y$ (closing prices)Price dimension
3Sum $x \times y$Covariance component
4Sum $x^2$Variance component
5$Slope = \frac{N \sum(xy) - \sum x \sum y}{N \sum(x^2) - (\sum x)^2}$Line gradient
6$Intercept = \frac{\sum y - Slope \times \sum x}{N}$Line y-intercept
7$LSMA = Intercept + Slope \times N$Endpoint at current bar

Mathematical Formulation

$$LSMA_t = \overline{C} + m \times (N - \overline{t})$$

Where:

- $\overline{C}$ = Average of closing prices

- $m$ = Regression slope

- $\overline{t}$ = Average of time indices

Real-World Analogy

Think of LSMA like drawing a ruler through your recent price data. Instead of averaging all the points (like SMA), you're drawing the best-fit line and asking: "If this trend continues linearly, where should price be today?" The LSMA tells you that projected value. This is why it often appears to "predict" turns—the best-fit line points in the direction data is actually heading.

Why These Values?

LSMA requires at least 2 data points (mathematically), but 25 provides a balance between smoothness and responsiveness. Unlike EMAs, LSMA doesn't have an inherent "lag period"—it computes a fresh regression each bar.

7. Practical Cautions

Lag Characteristics

ComponentCharacteristic
Inherent LagLower than SMA/EMA of same period
CalculationFresh regression each bar (no recursion lag)
SensitivityHigher than EMA; may overshoot
Reality Check: LSMA reduces lag but increases sensitivity to outliers. A single flash crash candle can violently skew the regression line for $N$ bars.

False Signal Scenarios

1. Outlier Distortion

Flash crash creates Lower Low, LSMA plunges, then data normalizes. The regression "remembers" the outlier for Period bars.

2. R-Squared Invalidation

If data points are scattered (low R-squared), the LSMA value is mathematically meaningless—a line fit to noise.

3. Overshoot in Curves

In parabolic rallies, LSMA undershoots because linear fit can't capture exponential curve.

Mitigation Strategies

- R-Squared Filter: Only trust LSMA when R² > 0.20

- Hook Confirmation: Wait for LSMA to "hook" (change slope) before acting on crossovers

- Outlier Exclusion: Some implementations trim extreme values before fitting

8. Pro Insights

LSMA vs EMA Crossover

A professional technique is to compare LSMA(25) with EMA(25). Since LSMA fits the regression and EMA smooths the average:

- When LSMA > EMA: Regression trend is stronger than the average—accelerating

- When LSMA < EMA: Regression trend is weaker—decelerating

This crossover often precedes major trend changes.

R-Squared as a Quality Metric

Plot R² alongside LSMA. When R² is high (>0.60), the regression fit is strong—LSMA signals are reliable. When R² is low (<0.20), the "trend" isn't really linear—ignore LSMA signals.

Divergence Trading

LSMA divergences are particularly powerful because they represent *statistical* divergence:

- Price makes Higher High but LSMA makes Lower High = The best-fit trajectory is decelerating despite price advancing

9. Summary

"LSMA is the market's trend vector—it draws the line of best fit through price and points in the direction the data truly wants to go."

PART 2: SUPPLEMENTARY GUIDE

1. Core Concept

LSMA answers: "If price is moving linearly, where should it be right now?"

Traditional moving averages assume the "average" represents fair value. LSMA assumes the market is *moving in a direction* and asks what the current target is *if that movement continues*.

Mathematically, LSMA fits $y = mx + b$ through the data:

- $m$ (slope) = Direction and speed

- $b$ (intercept) = Starting point

- Endpoint = Where the line reaches at the current bar

This endpoint updates each bar as new data shifts the best-fit line.

2. Statistical Edge

Why It Works

LSMA provides edge through reduced lag compared to SMA/EMA. The regression endpoint account for the *slope* of data, not just the average.

MetricSMA(25)EMA(25)LSMA(25)
Lag~12 bars~8 bars~3-5 bars
Curve FittingPoorModerateLinear only
Outlier SensitivityLowLowHigh

When Regression Works

LSMA works best when R² is high—meaning the data actually fits a line. In choppy markets (R² < 0.15), the regression line oscillates meaninglessly.

3. Actionable Strategy

Strategy Name: "Regression Momentum Cross"

Concept: Use LSMA for trigger, MACD for confirmation.

Setup Requirements:

1. LSMA(25) slope is positive

2. MACD Histogram is positive

3. R-Squared(25) > 0.25

Entry Trigger:

- Price crosses above LSMA(25)

- All setup conditions met

Position Sizing:

- Full size when R² > 0.50

- Half size when R² 0.25-0.50

Exit Rules:

1. Primary Exit: Price crosses below LSMA

2. Stop Loss: 2× ATR below LSMA

3. Take Profit: LSMA slope flattens to near-zero

5. Further Study

Creator Attribution

LSMA is an application of Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression—developed by Carl Friedrich Gauss and Adrien-Marie Legendre in the early 19th century—to financial time series. Its adoption in trading accelerated with computerization in the 1980s-90s.

PART 3: ADVANCED SIGNAL REFERENCE

Advanced Signal Reference

Signal Patterns

LSMA Buy Signal

This signal requires three conditions: price crossing above LSMA, positive slope (indicating upward trend trajectory), and statistically significant R-squared value. The R² threshold ensures the regression fit is meaningful—trading when R² < 0.20 means trading on random noise rather than actual trend.


def lsma_buy_signal(price, lsma, lsma_prev, r_squared):
    crossover = price > lsma and price_prev <= lsma_prev
    slope_positive = lsma > lsma_prev
    statistically_valid = r_squared > 0.25
    
    return crossover and slope_positive and statistically_valid
