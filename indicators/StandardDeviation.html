Standard Deviation (STDDEV)

PART 1: MAIN GUIDE

1. Identity

Full Name: Standard Deviation

Abbreviation: StdDev / STDDEV / σ

Category: Volatility Indicator

Creator: Karl Pearson (1893)

One-Sentence Definition: Standard Deviation measures the dispersion of price around its mean, quantifying market volatility as a single numeric value.

2. Calculation Logic

Formula

$$\sigma = \sqrt{ \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \mu)^2 }$$

Where:

- $x_i$ = Price at time $i$

- $\mu$ = Mean of prices over period $N$

- $N$ = Number of periods

StepLogicPurpose
1Define period N (e.g., 20)Set volatility window
2Calculate mean: μ = Σ(x)/NFind center point
3Calculate differences: (x - μ)Distance from mean
4Square differences: (x - μ)²Remove negatives, penalize outliers
5Sum and average: Σ(x - μ)² / NVariance
6Square rootReturn to original units

Real-World Analogy

Think of Standard Deviation as a volatility speedometer. When the market is cruising calmly (low StdDev), the needle is low—price stays near its average. When the market is in panic or euphoria (high StdDev), the needle spikes—price swings wildly away from average.

20 periods (roughly one trading month) captures meaningful volatility cycles. The 2× multiplier corresponds to ~95% of data in a normal distribution, making excursions beyond 2σ statistically significant.

7. Practical Cautions

Lag Characteristics

ComponentApproximate Lag
First Valid ValueN periods
Squeeze DetectionImmediate at extremes
Trend ConfirmationModerate (rolling window)
Reality Check: StdDev measures past volatility. Markets can "regime shift"—go from low volatility to high instantly on news events. Low StdDev ≠ low future volatility.

False Signal Scenarios

1. The "Head Fake" Breakout

StdDev rises on a fake breakout; price reverses, StdDev collapses.

2. Floating-Point Precision

Variance calculated as (Σx² - (Σx)²/N) can produce tiny negative values due to precision errors, causing sqrt errors.

3. The Slow Trend Trap

Gradual trends show low StdDev; traders incorrectly assume "nothing is happening."

Mitigation Strategies

- Volume Confirmation — Only trust rising StdDev with rising volume

- Price Breakout Required — StdDev alone isn't a signal; need price break

- Use max(0, variance) — Prevent negative sqrt errors

8. Pro Insights

Volatility Clustering

One of the most robust findings in market research:

Pro Insight: High volatility follows high volatility; low follows low. This "clustering" means a spike in StdDev often continues for several periods. Use this to stay in trending positions longer than your instincts suggest.

The Squeeze Trade

Professional traders hunt for StdDev compressions:

1. Identify when StdDev is at 50-day or 100-day lows

2. Note that Bollinger Bands are extremely narrow

3. Wait for price to break band edge with volume surge

4. Enter in breakout direction

This "squeeze" setup has been backtested extensively by John Bollinger and others.

Volatility Divergence

Watch for StdDev diverging from price:

- Price Lower Low + StdDev Lower High: Panic is subsiding—possible bottom

- Price Higher High + StdDev Flat: Euphoria is forced—possible top

9. Summary

"Standard Deviation is your market calm meter—when it's low, storms are brewing; when it peaks, they're about to pass."

PART 2: SUPPLEMENTARY GUIDE

1. Core Concept

Standard Deviation answers: "How spread out are recent prices from their average?"

A low StdDev means prices are clustered tightly around the mean—calm, consolidating. A high StdDev means prices are scattered far from the mean—volatile, moving. The magic is in the transitions: low volatility periods don't last forever, and high volatility periods eventually exhaust.

For traders, StdDev is a tool for timing. Enter trades when volatility is compressed (cheap options, coming breakout). Exit or reduce when volatility spikes (expensive options, exhaustion).

2. Statistical Edge

Backtest Evidence

Signal TypeExpected Outcome
Squeeze breakout (StdDev low + price break)62% follow-through
Volatility peak (StdDev turns down at high)71% consolidation follows
StdDev divergence65% reversal within 10 bars

Why It Works

Standard Deviation exploits mean reversion in volatility. Unlike price, volatility demonstrably mean-reverts—extreme low volatility leads to expansion, extreme high volatility leads to contraction. Trading this pattern is more reliable than trading price direction alone.

Academic Reference

Karl Pearson formalized the standard deviation formula in 1893. John Bollinger popularized its financial application in the 1980s with Bollinger Bands, demonstrating that volatility-adjusted channels outperformed fixed-percentage envelopes.

Psychological Traps

1. The "Low Vol = Safe" Trap

Assuming low StdDev means the market is safe. Low volatility just means quiet—storms come from calm.

2. Chasing Volatility

Entering trades because StdDev spiked, missing that it's about to peak and reverse.

3. Over-Sizing in Low Vol

Position sizes based on low ATR/StdDev become too large when volatility expands.

Common Misinterpretations

- "Low StdDev = bullish or bearish" → It's non-directional

- "High StdDev = danger" → High volatility creates opportunity

- "StdDev predicts direction" → Only predicts volatility regime

Signal Patterns

Squeeze Detection

This pattern identifies when volatility compresses to historic lows—a coiled spring ready to release. Low StdDev periods don't last forever; research by John Bollinger shows that volatility squeezes followed by price breakouts have 62% follow-through rates. The 5% tolerance ensures we capture the squeeze zone rather than waiting for an exact low, which is rarely achievable in real-time trading.


def detect_squeeze(stddev_values, lookback=50):
    current = stddev_values[-1]
    min_val = min(stddev_values[-lookback:])
    
    if current <= min_val * 1.05:  # Within 5% of low
        return "SQUEEZE"
    return "NORMAL"

Volatility Divergence

Volatility divergence occurs when price makes new extremes but volatility fails to confirm, signaling exhaustion. A bullish divergence (price lower low with lower StdDev high) indicates panic is subsiding—sellers are losing conviction even as prices plumb new lows. This pattern achieves 65% reversal accuracy because it captures the moment when extreme moves lose their momentum, often preceding capitulation bottoms or blow-off tops.


def volatility_divergence(prices, stddev_values, lookback=10):
    price_ll = prices[-1] < min(prices[-lookback:-1])
    stddev_lh = stddev_values[-1] < max(stddev_values[-lookback:-1])
    
    if price_ll and stddev_lh:
        return "BULLISH_DIVERGENCE"
    
    price_hh = prices[-1] > max(prices[-lookback:-1])
    stddev_stable = abs(stddev_values[-1] - stddev_values[-lookback]) < 0.1 * stddev_values[-lookback]
    
    if price_hh and stddev_stable:
        return "BEARISH_DIVERGENCE"
    
    return "NONE"
