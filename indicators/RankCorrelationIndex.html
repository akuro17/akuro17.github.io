Rank Correlation Index (RCI)

PART 1: MAIN GUIDE

1. Identity

Full Name: Rank Correlation Index (Spearman's Rank Correlation)

Abbreviation: RCI

Category: Japanese Cycle / Statistical Oscillator

Creator: Charles Spearman (Math, 1904); Japanese Technical Analysts (Trading Application, Late 20th Century)

One-Sentence Definition: RCI measures the correlation between the chronological order of days and the price rank order, revealing how perfectly prices are trending without being distorted by price magnitude.

2. Calculation Logic

Formula

$$RCI = \left( 1 - \frac{6 \sum_{i=1}^{N} d_i^2}{N(N^2 - 1)} \right) \times 100$$

Where:

- $N$ = Lookback period (e.g., 9, 26, or 52 days)

- $d_i$ = Difference between Time Rank and Price Rank for day $i$

- The constant $6$ normalizes the sum of squared differences

StepLogicPurpose
1Assign Time Rank (1 = most recent, N = oldest)Chronological ordering
2Sort prices, assign Price Rank (1 = highest, N = lowest)Magnitude ordering
3Calculate $d = TimeRank - PriceRank$ for each dayMeasure misalignment
4Square differences, sum themAggregate deviation
5Apply Spearman formulaNormalize to [-100, +100]

Real-World Analogy

Think of RCI like a marathon rank tracker. If runners finish in exactly the order they started (race position = starting number), correlation is perfect (+100). If they finish in reverse order, correlation is perfectly negative (-100). Random finishes produce correlation near zero. RCI asks: "Are recent days finishing with the top prices?"

7. Practical Cautions

Lag Characteristics

ComponentApproximate Lag
RCI(9) First Value9 bars
RCI(26) First Value26 bars
RCI(52) First Value52 bars
Signal ResponsivenessImmediate at extremes
Reality Check: RCI can remain "pegged" at +100 or -100 during strong trends. A reading of +100 simply means price has risen perfectly for N days—it doesn't mean the trend will reverse immediately.

False Signal Scenarios

1. The Unpinning Trap

RCI drops from +100 to +90. This isn't necessarily bearish—it might just be one day disrupting perfect correlation.

2. Choppy Rank Noise

In sideways markets, rank orders shuffle randomly, producing oscillating RCI with no predictive value.

3. Magnitude Ignorance

A 5% daily move and 0.01% daily move can produce identical RCI if they maintain the same rank order.

Mitigation Strategies

- Trade Only at Extremes: Ignore RCI between -80 and +80

- Trend Shield Rule: Trade only in direction of RCI(26) slope

- Multi-Timeframe Confirmation: Wait for Short and Medium RCI alignment

8. Pro Insights

The "Hook" Signal

When RCI(9) reaches +100 (perfect uptrend correlation), stays "pegged" for several days, then sharply hooks down, this "unpinning" is one of the fastest exit signals available. It triggers before moving average crossovers and often before price shows visible weakness.

Triple Line Bunching

The most powerful RCI signal occurs when all three lines (9, 26, 52) converge at an extreme (above +80 or below -80) and then turn simultaneously. This "cycle resonance" indicates short, medium, and long-term trends are all overextended and reversing together—extremely rare but high probability.

Time-Price Warp

Unlike other oscillators, RCI's *slope* matters more than its *level*. If RCI(52) is at -50 but sloping upward, the bias is bullish. The long-term cycle is turning positive even though the absolute reading seems neutral.

9. Summary

"RCI measures whether price is rising in lockstep with time—perfect correlation signals a pristine trend, while breakdown signals cycle exhaustion."

PART 2: SUPPLEMENTARY GUIDE

1. Core Concept

RCI answers one question: "How perfectly is price trending?"

Most oscillators measure *how much* price moved (magnitude). RCI measures *how consistently* price moved (order). If a stock closes higher each successive day for 9 days, RCI(9) = +100 regardless of whether gains were 0.1% or 10% daily.

The calculation ranks prices and times, then measures correlation:

- Perfect uptrend (+100): Today is highest, yesterday second highest, etc.

- Perfect downtrend (-100): Today is lowest, yesterday second lowest, etc.

- Random walk (0): No relationship between time and price rank

This "topological" view is immune to outliers—a flash crash that immediately recovers affects magnitude indicators but barely touches RCI.

2. Statistical Edge

Why It Works

The Spearman correlation coefficient is non-parametric—it doesn't assume normal distribution. This makes it robust to fat tails and outliers that plague traditional momentum indicators. When RCI reaches extremes, it reflects a statistically improbable run that typically regresses.

Academic Reference

Charles Spearman developed the rank correlation coefficient in 1904 for psychological research. Japanese traders recognized its application to markets: time is the independent variable, price is dependent. If markets were random walks, RCI would hover around zero.

Failure Patterns

Tie DistortionIdentical prices produce ambiguous ranksImplementation must handle ties properly
Computation ComplexitySorting each bar is expensivePre-optimize for real-time use

Psychological Traps

1. The "+100 Is Overbought" Trap

+100 means perfect correlation, not necessarily reversal. Strong trends maintain +100 for extended periods.

2. Expecting Magnitude

RCI ignoring price magnitude is a feature, not a bug. Accept it or pair with magnitude-based indicators.

3. Overcomplicating the Triple

Using all three lines (9, 26, 52) can lead to analysis paralysis. Simplify to Short + Medium.

Common Misinterpretations

- "+100 means sell" → It means perfect trend; reversal needs the "hook"

- "RCI is like RSI" → RSI measures gain/loss ratio; RCI measures rank correlation

- "Near zero is equilibrium" → Zero means randomness, not balance

Signal Patterns

Buy Signal (Oversold Hook)

This signal triggers when short-term RCI "hooks" upward after reaching extreme oversold territory (below -80), while medium-term RCI slope confirms the broader trend. The hook pattern—rising from extreme lows—is RCI's strongest reversal signal, indicating the selling cycle is exhausting. This pattern achieves ~58% accuracy at extreme levels according to historical testing.


def rci_buy_signal(rci_short, rci_mid):
    condition_1 = rci_short[-2] < -80          # Was oversold
    condition_2 = rci_short[-1] > rci_short[-2] # Hooking up
    condition_3 = rci_mid[-1] > rci_mid[-3]    # Medium-term slope positive
    
    return all([condition_1, condition_2, condition_3])

Sell Signal (Overbought Hook)

This exit signal fires when short-term RCI "hooks" downward after reaching extreme overbought territory (above +80), indicating the buying cycle has peaked. RCI at +80-100 means price has risen nearly perfectly in lockstep with time—a statistically improbable run that typically regresses. The hook down often precedes moving average crossovers and price weakness.


def rci_sell_signal(rci_short, rci_mid):
    condition_1 = rci_short[-2] > 80           # Was overbought
    condition_2 = rci_short[-1] < rci_short[-2] # Hooking down
    
    return all([condition_1, condition_2])

Triple Convergence Signal

This is RCI's highest-probability signal: all three lines (9, 26, 52) converging at an extreme and turning simultaneously. When short, medium, and long-term cycles all reach oversold together and reverse, it indicates a rare "cycle resonance" event. This pattern historically achieves significantly higher success rates than single-timeframe signals.


def rci_triple_convergence(rci_9, rci_26, rci_52, threshold=-80):
    # All three lines below threshold and turning up
    all_oversold = all([rci_9[-1] < threshold, 
                        rci_26[-1] < threshold, 
                        rci_52[-1] < threshold])
    all_turning = all([rci_9[-1] > rci_9[-2],
                       rci_26[-1] > rci_26[-2],
                       rci_52[-1] > rci_52[-2]])
    
    return all_oversold and all_turning
